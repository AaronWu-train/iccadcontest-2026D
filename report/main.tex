\section{Introduction}
Timing closure is a critical task in modern VLSI design. As clock frequency increases, setup and hold violations become harder to fix without disturbing the functional logic of the circuit. ICCAD Contest 2026 Problem D focuses on this setting: given an initial clock tree, a buffer library, and data-path delay reports, the goal is to improve timing quality by modifying the clock tree while preserving the original data-path logic.

The key idea behind this problem is useful skew. Instead of treating all clock skew as harmful, useful-skew optimization intentionally changes clock arrival times at flip-flops to improve timing margins. For a setup-critical path, delaying the capture clock can provide more time for data propagation. For a hold-critical path, delaying the launch clock can postpone data launch and reduce the risk of early data arrival. Therefore, by inserting or resizing buffers in the clock tree, the optimizer can improve setup and hold slack without changing combinational logic.

However, this optimization is highly coupled. A clock-tree edit does not affect only one data path; it changes the clock arrival time of an entire downstream subtree. As a result, a move that improves one violating path may degrade other paths that share the same clock-tree nodes. In addition, buffer insertion and upsizing may increase area, while aggressive area recovery may undo previous timing improvements. The objective is therefore not simply to fix the worst violation, but to balance SS setup improvement, FF hold improvement, and clock-tree area.

In this work, we formulate timing fixing by useful skew as a constrained local-search problem over legal clock-tree states. Our solver maintains a mutable clock-tree representation, evaluates timing and area metrics after local edits, and uses reversible trial operations to explore the search space efficiently. Candidate-generation policies decide which clock-tree edits should be evaluated, while acceptance strategies decide how the solver moves from one tree state to another. The experimental discussion focuses on how these policies trade off setup slack, hold slack, and area.

The rest of this report is organized as follows. Section 2 formulates the timing model, objective, and legal action space. Section 3 describes the local-search framework, including candidate generation, action evaluation, acceptance strategies, and legality checks. Section 4 reports experimental results and discusses the trade-offs among the tested optimizers. Section 5 concludes the report and outlines future improvements.


\section{Problem Formulation}

\subsection{Input Design and Legal Solution Space}

Each testcase consists of an initial clock tree, a buffer library, and two data-path delay reports under the SS and FF corners. The clock tree contains a clock source as the root, clock buffers as internal nodes, and flip-flops as leaf nodes. The buffer library specifies the available buffer types, including their area, maximum fanout constraint, and fanout-dependent delay under each timing corner. The delay reports give the clock period and the fixed data-path delay between launch and capture flip-flops.

The optimizer is allowed to improve timing only by modifying the clock tree. The data-path delay is fixed, so the combinational logic is not part of the optimization space. A legal output must preserve all original contest nodes and their names, and the relative order among original nodes in the clock tree must remain unchanged. Newly inserted buffers must have unique names, all pins must be connected, each flip-flop must have a single driver, and every buffer must satisfy the fanout limit specified by the buffer library.

We model a solution as a clock-tree state. A state records the cell type of each buffer, the parent-child connectivity of the clock tree, and the set of buffers inserted by the optimizer. The legal solution space consists of all clock trees reachable from the input tree by legal clock-tree edits while satisfying the structural constraints above.

\subsection{Clock Arrival Time and Useful Skew}
For each flip-flop, the clock arrival time is the total delay from the clock source to that flip-flop through the clock tree. Since buffer delay depends on the selected cell type, the fanout count, and the timing corner, the clock arrival time is also corner-dependent.

A data path connects a launch flip-flop to a capture flip-flop through combinational logic. Let $D_{clk}^{\mathrm{launch}}$ and $D_{clk}^{\mathrm{capture}}$ denote the clock arrival times from the clock source to the launch and capture flip-flops. We define clock skew as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Skew} = D_{clk}^{\mathrm{capture}} - D_{clk}^{\mathrm{launch}}.
\end{equation}
With this convention, positive skew means that the capture clock arrives later than the launch clock. A clock-tree edit may change the arrival time of an entire downstream subtree, so the skew of many data paths can change after a single local modification.
\subsection{Setup/Hold Slack and Timing Metrics}

Let $D_{\mathrm{data}}$ be the data-path delay, $T_{\mathrm{clk}}$ be the clock period, $T_{\mathrm{setup}}$ be the setup requirement, and $T_{\mathrm{hold}}$ be the hold requirement.  With the skew convention above, the setup and hold slacks are
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{Slack}_{\mathrm{setup}}
  &= T_{\mathrm{clk}} - T_{\mathrm{setup}} - D_{\mathrm{data}} + \mathrm{Skew}, \\
  \mathrm{Slack}_{\mathrm{hold}}
  &= D_{\mathrm{data}} - T_{\mathrm{hold}} - \mathrm{Skew}.
\end{align}

A negative setup slack means that data arrives too late at the capture flip-flop.  A negative hold slack means that data arrives too early and may overwrite the previous value.  Therefore, a setup violation can often be improved by increasing the capture clock delay, while a hold violation can often be improved by increasing the launch clock delay.

Timing quality is measured using total negative slack (TNS) and worst negative slack (WNS).  WNS captures the most severe timing violation among all paths, while TNS measures the accumulated amount of negative slack.  In this problem, setup timing is evaluated under the setup-oriented SS corner, and hold timing is evaluated under the hold-oriented FF corner.

\subsection{Timing-Area Objective and Internal Proxy Score}
The objective combines timing and area.  Timing quality is measured by TNS and WNS under the SS and FF corners, while area is the total area of the clock-tree buffers.  Given a baseline metric value $x^{\mathrm{ori}}$ and a final value $x$, we use the relative improvement form
\begin{equation}
I(x, x^{\mathrm{ori}}) = 1 - \frac{x}{x^{\mathrm{ori}}},
\end{equation}
when $x^{\mathrm{ori}}$ is nonzero.  This form rewards reducing negative timing metrics toward zero and rewards reducing area below the baseline.

The official contest score is a weighted sum of setup improvement, hold improvement, and area reduction:

\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Score} &= \alpha \left[ \left( 1 - \frac{\mathrm{TNS}_{SS}}{\mathrm{TNS}_{SS}^{ori}} \right) + \left( 1 - \frac{\mathrm{WNS}_{SS}}{\mathrm{WNS}_{SS}^{ori}} \right) \right] + \beta \left[ \left( 1 - \frac{\mathrm{TNS}_{FF}}{\mathrm{TNS}_{FF}^{ori}} \right) + \left( 1 - \frac{\mathrm{WNS}_{FF}}{\mathrm{WNS}_{FF}^{ori}} \right) \right] \\ &+ \gamma \left( 1 - \frac{\mathrm{Area}}{\mathrm{Area}^{ori}} \right).
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The official weights are not disclosed, so the optimizer cannot directly use the official score as its search objective.  According to the Q\&A session, the organizers indicated that the weights roughly satisfy $\alpha > \beta \approx \gamma$.  Based on this hint, we use a proxy score with fixed weights $\alpha = 0.5$, $\beta = 0.25$, and $\gamma = 0.25$ to guide move selection during optimization.  These parameters are not claimed to be the hidden official weights; they are used only as an internal ranking function that reflects the relative importance of setup repair, hold repair, and area control.

This objective captures the main trade-off in the problem.  Aggressive buffer insertion or upsizing may improve setup or hold slack, but the same move can reduce the area component and may also hurt timing on other paths.  On the other hand, area recovery may remove unnecessary delay but can also undo a previous timing repair.  Therefore, the optimizer must balance timing improvement and area cost rather than optimizing only one metric independently.

\subsection{Local Edit Semantics}

Our optimizer explores the solution space using three local edit primitives during search.  First, an \emph{insert} move places a new buffer on an existing clock-tree edge.  This increases the clock delay of the downstream subtree and can be used to adjust useful skew, but it also increases area.

Second, a \emph{resize} move changes the cell type of an existing buffer. Resizing gives a finer way to increase or decrease clock delay and area without changing the clock-tree topology.  Depending on the selected cell type, a resize move may either improve timing by adding delay or reduce area by using a smaller buffer.

Third, a \emph{remove} move deletes a buffer that was inserted by the optimizer in an earlier step and reconnects the surrounding tree.  This move is used only as an internal search operation for reversible exploration and area recovery. Original contest nodes remain part of the design and are never removed.  Thus, the final output still satisfies the legal modification constraints of the problem.

Because the number of possible edit sequences is large, the solver cannot enumerate the full state space.  Instead, each iteration generates a smaller set of candidate moves and evaluates their impact on the current timing and proxy score.  The rest of the report describes how we construct these candidate sets and how different search policies decide which move to accept.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Solver Overview}

Our solver treats useful-skew optimization as a constrained local-search
problem.  Starting from the input clock tree, it repeatedly evaluates legal
nearby states and moves through the search space according to a candidate policy
and an acceptance strategy.  The candidate policy decides which local clock-tree
edits are worth evaluating, while the acceptance strategy decides whether an
evaluated edit should become the next state.

Figure~\ref{fig:flowchart} shows the overall structure of our solver.
The loop maintains both the current state and the best state found so far.  When
the time budget is reached, the solver restores the best-seen clock tree and
writes it as the final output.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:local-search} gives the corresponding optimization
procedure.  The rest of this section explains the implementation details that
support this flow: incremental timing evaluation, candidate generation,
acceptance strategies, and output-format validation.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
  \caption{Overall local-search optimization flow}
  \label{alg:local-search}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State Build the initial \texttt{ClockTree}, \texttt{DataPathGraph}, and \texttt{TimingState}
    \State Evaluate the baseline tree and compute its proxy score
    \State $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cur}} \gets$ baseline clock tree
    \State $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{best}} \gets \mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cur}}$
    \State $S_{\mathrm{best}} \gets S_{\mathrm{proxy}}(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cur}})$

\While{current time < time budget}
\State Generate a candidate move set $\mathcal{M}$ using the candidate policy
\State $\mathcal{E} \gets \emptyset$

\ForAll{$m \in \mathcal{M}$}
\State Temporarily apply $m$ to $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cur}}$
\State Update timing and area metrics
\State Compute $\Delta S_{\mathrm{proxy}}(m)$
\State Store the evaluated move information in $\mathcal{E}$
\State Undo $m$ and restore $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cur}}$
\EndFor

\State Select a move $m^\star$ from $\mathcal{E}$ using the acceptance strategy

\If{$m^\star$ exists}
\State Apply $m^\star$ to $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cur}}$
\State Update \texttt{TimingState} for the current tree

\If{$S_{\mathrm{proxy}}(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cur}}) > S_{\mathrm{best}}$}
\State $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{best}} \gets \mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cur}}$
\State $S_{\mathrm{best}} \gets S_{\mathrm{proxy}}(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{cur}})$
\EndIf
\EndIf

\EndWhile

\State Restore $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{best}}$
\State Perform full timing evaluation
\State Write \texttt{modified\_clk\_tree.structure}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Optimization State and Incremental Evaluation}

The optimizer uses three data structures during search.  The \texttt{ClockTree}
is the mutable solution state.  It stores the active topology, buffer cell
types, node origins, and stable node/edge identifiers.  The
\texttt{DataPathGraph} stores the fixed launch flip-flop, capture flip-flop, and
data delay of each timing path.  Since useful-skew optimization changes only the
clock tree, the data-path graph remains read-only.  The \texttt{TimingState}
connects these two structures and caches the quantities needed for fast search:
clock arrivals, setup and hold slacks, TNS, WNS, area, and the proxy score.

Candidate scoring is performed by temporary application.  For a trial edit, the
solver first applies the edit to the \texttt{ClockTree}.  The
\texttt{TimingState} then updates only the affected downstream clock arrivals
and the timing paths incident to affected flip-flops.  After the proxy-score
change is computed, the edit is either kept or rolled back.  Rollback restores
both the clock-tree topology and the timing cache, so rejected candidates do not
pollute later evaluations.

This design avoids recomputing the whole timing graph for every trial move.  The
full \texttt{evaluate()} routine is still kept as the authoritative path: it
recomputes all arrivals and slacks from scratch at the beginning and at the end
of optimization.  During the inner search loop, however, \texttt{TimingState}
provides the faster incremental approximation needed to evaluate many local
edits under a fixed time budget.

\subsection{Candidate Generation}

The legal edit space is too large to enumerate at every iteration.  Therefore,
the solver constructs a bounded candidate set that represents a useful local
neighborhood around the current tree.  Different candidate policies emphasize
different ways of finding useful skew.

\paragraph{Random.}
The random policy samples moves from the legal action space with predefined
probabilities.  It may generate buffer insertions, inserted-buffer removals, or
buffer resizes.  Although it is not directly guided by timing violations, it
provides broad exploration and can find non-obvious area-recovery or resize
moves.

\paragraph{Violation Path.}
The violation-path policy scans all data paths and selects paths with negative
slack, prioritizing the most severe violations.  If a setup constraint is
violated, the policy generates moves near the capture flip-flop, since changing
the capture-side clock delay can improve setup slack.  If a hold constraint is
violated, the policy generates moves near the launch flip-flop, since changing
the launch-side clock delay can improve hold slack.

\paragraph{Upstream Window.}
The upstream-window policy starts from a violation endpoint and explores several
buffers toward the upstream side of the clock tree.  This policy creates more
candidate moves than the violation-path policy and can discover edits that
affect multiple related paths.  However, it is also more time-consuming because
it evaluates a larger local neighborhood.

\paragraph{Critical Endpoint.}
The critical-endpoint policy assigns a criticality value to each flip-flop based
on how much it contributes to total negative slack.  Negative setup slack is
added to the criticality of the capture flip-flop, while negative hold slack is
added to the criticality of the launch flip-flop.  The policy then generates
moves around the flip-flops with the largest criticality, aiming to repair
violations on multiple data paths at once.

\paragraph{Union Pool.}
The union-pool policy combines the focused insertion policies with remove and
resize candidates.  Duplicate candidates are removed before evaluation.  This
policy gives the acceptance strategy a broader view of the local neighborhood:
it can compare direct timing-repair moves with area-recovery and buffer-sizing
moves in the same iteration.

\paragraph{Sampled Union Pool.}
The sampled-union-pool policy uses the same move families as the union pool, but
it samples one family probabilistically and then samples one candidate from that
family.  This produces a cheaper stochastic proposal for simulated-annealing
based optimizers, where evaluating a full candidate batch at every iteration
would spend too much of the time budget on scoring.

\subsection{Acceptance Strategies}

Candidate generation controls which edits are considered.  Acceptance controls
how the search moves after those edits have been scored.  We compare five
acceptance strategies, each using the same incremental apply-score-undo
mechanism described above.

\paragraph{Greedy.}
Given the candidates generated by the selected candidate policy, the greedy
strategy applies each candidate temporarily and computes its proxy-score change
$\Delta S_{\mathrm{proxy}}$.  It accepts only moves with
$\Delta S_{\mathrm{proxy}} > 0$ and chooses the move with the largest positive
improvement.  After the main greedy steps, the implementation also performs
bounded resize-polish steps to recover additional score through buffer sizing.
This strategy is simple and stable, but it may get stuck in a local optimum.

\paragraph{Two-Step Optimization.}
The two-step strategy separates the search into two objective phases.  The first
phase accepts moves that improve a slack-focused objective, with stronger weight
on setup TNS and WNS.  The second phase switches back to the full proxy score,
so area and hold timing are considered together with setup timing.  This design
follows the intuition that the search can first create timing margin and then
recover score through more balanced moves.

\paragraph{Simulated Annealing (SA).}
Simulated annealing is a stochastic local-search method that can accept worse
solutions with a temperature-controlled probability~\cite{kirkpatrick1983optimization}.
In our optimizer, SA samples one proposal, evaluates its proxy-score change, and
accepts all improving moves.  A worsening move may still be accepted according
to the Metropolis probability.  The optimizer tracks the best state separately
and restores it when the search becomes stale or drifts too far from the best
score.

\paragraph{Iterated Simulated Annealing (ISA).}
Iterated simulated annealing splits the remaining time into several SA rounds.
Between rounds, the solver restores the best state and runs small greedy cleanup
batches.  The SA phases provide exploration, while the greedy batches exploit
local improvements around promising states.

\paragraph{Tabu Search.}
Tabu search uses short-term memory to avoid cycling among recently used
moves~\cite{glover1989tabu}.  At each step, the optimizer evaluates the
candidate set and chooses the best non-tabu move.  A tabu move can still be
accepted by aspiration if it improves the global best score.  After the time or
step limit is reached, the optimizer restores the best-seen tree before
returning it to the solver.

The A1--A13 optimizers evaluated in Section~4 are combinations of these
candidate-generation policies and acceptance strategies.  This separation lets
us compare whether performance differences come mainly from the neighborhood
definition or from the way the search accepts moves.

\subsection{Output Format Validation}

The final solver output must be a legal \texttt{modified\_clk\_tree.structure}
file, not only a high-scoring internal state.  For this reason, the same
\texttt{ClockTree} model is used for optimization and for writing the output.
After the selected optimizer finishes, the solver holds the best-seen clock tree
and writes it through a preorder traversal with depth information, matching the
format of the input clock-tree structure.

Output validity is protected during move construction.  Inserted buffers receive
unique generated names, resize and insertion moves are rejected when the chosen
cell does not support the required fanout, and remove moves are limited to
optimizer-inserted buffers.  Original contest nodes remain alive and keep their
names.  Search-only buffers that were removed are marked inactive and are skipped
by the output traversal, so they do not appear in the final structure file.

The writer emits only active nodes and their active parent-child relationships.
Therefore, each flip-flop keeps a single clock-tree parent, the tree connectivity
remains well formed, and every emitted buffer has a cell type that satisfies the
fanout table in the buffer library.  The final write uses a temporary file
followed by a rename, so the output path is replaced only after a complete
structure file has been produced.

\section{Experimental Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Using Different Candidate Policy}
We first compare different candidate policies under the greedy algorithm.

\textbf{Score Analysis.}
Overall, union pool is the best candidate policy, while random and upstream window are close behind.  Violation path and critical endpoint perform worse because they do not improve both setup and hold slack sufficiently.  The stronger policies have similar setup performance, but union pool gains extra score from better hold improvement and more controlled area.

\medskip

\textbf{Possible Reason.}
Greedy search always chooses the best immediately improving move.  Therefore, its performance depends heavily on whether the candidate pool contains a good local edit.  Violation path and critical endpoint generate narrower candidate sets, so they are more likely to miss useful upstream or area-recovery moves.


\subsection{Using Different Accept Policy}
We next compare accept policies using random candidates, union pool, and sampled union pool.
\subsubsection{Random Candidate}
With random candidates, tabu search gives the best result, while two-step optimization is the weakest.  The main issue of two-step optimization is that its first stage focuses on timing repair and its second stage tries to recover area afterward.  This separation is too rigid: area-recovery moves may undo previous timing repair, while timing-focused moves may leave too much area cost for the second stage to clean up.  As a result, two-step optimization has weaker hold slack and area than SA, ISA, and tabu search.  Its timing-first stage also does not outperform SA/ISA enough to justify the later cleanup cost.  Tabu search performs better because it can accept non-improving intermediate moves, avoid recent cycles with tabu memory, and still restore the best solution found.

\subsubsection{Union Pool and Sampled Union Pool}
With union pool and sampled union pool, two-step optimization is still the weakest.  Although union pool provides more candidates, the two fixed stages do not convert this larger search space into a better final score.  SA and ISA aggressively improve setup slack, but they pay a large area penalty because many accepted moves insert or preserve extra buffers.  Tabu search is more balanced: it improves timing while keeping the final area close to the baseline.

\subsection{Difference Between Random and (Sampled) Union Pool on ISA and Tabu}
The best strategy is tabu search with random candidates.  ISA with sampled union pool improves setup slack the most, but this improvement is obtained at the cost of much larger area.  In contrast, tabu search with random candidates still improves setup slack significantly, achieves the strongest hold improvement, and reduces area consumption.  Therefore, its advantage comes from the best trade-off among setup, hold, and area rather than from maximizing a single component.

\subsubsection{Possible Reason}
ISA and tabu search are both designed to escape local optima.  Random candidates provide wider exploration, while tabu memory prevents the search from repeatedly returning to recently used moves.  This also explains why \textit{tabu-random} outperforms \textit{tabu-union-pool}: union pool is more timing-aware, but it can bias the search toward violation-related edits, while random candidates expose more non-local resize and removal opportunities.  This combination works better than a purely timing-focused sampled union proposal when area must also be controlled.


\section{Conclusion}
In this work, we formulate timing fixing by useful skew as a clock-tree optimization problem over three reversible operations: buffer insertion, inserted buffer removal, and buffer resizing.  These operations allow the optimizer to change clock arrival times at flip-flops and improve setup or hold slack without modifying the data-path logic.  Since each edit may affect multiple paths and also changes area, the main challenge is not only timing repair, but the balance among setup slack, hold slack, and area cost.

Our experiments show that both candidate generation and acceptance strategy have a significant impact on the final result.  Greedy search benefits from a larger candidate pool because it only accepts immediately improving moves.  In contrast, simulated annealing and tabu search can explore beyond local optima, which makes random candidate generation more useful.  Among all tested optimizers, tabu search with random candidates achieves the best average score. It does not produce the highest setup component, but it achieves the strongest hold improvement while also reducing area, giving the best overall trade-off. SA and ISA can aggressively improve timing, especially setup slack, but their area penalty reduces the final score.  Two-step optimization follows a reasonable timing-first and area-recovery idea, but its fixed phase separation does not recover enough hold slack and area to compete with tabu search.

For future work, an adaptive strategy could select candidate policies according to the current violation pattern instead of using a fixed policy throughout the run.  Another promising direction is to keep the exploration strength of tabu search while adding a stronger area-aware resize and removal polish in the late stage.  This may preserve the timing gains from useful skew while further reducing unnecessary buffer area.
